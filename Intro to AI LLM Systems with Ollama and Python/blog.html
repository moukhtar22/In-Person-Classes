**Reading time: 2 minutes** Over the past decade, mass shootings have made school safety a top priority. Many districts now employ surveillance software that scans students’ online activity for threats. In Florida, that system has just intercepted a disturbing request. **The incident** At Southwestern Middle School in Deland, a 13‑year‑old student typed into OpenAI’s ChatGPT: “how to kill my friend in the middle of class.” The query triggered an alert on Gaggle’s monitoring platform, which tracks keyword‑based activity on school‑issued computers. Local police were immediately notified. The teenager later told the Volusia County Sheriff’s Office that he was “just trolling” a friend who had annoyed him, but the sheriff’s office called the request “another joke that created an emergency on campus.” The student was arrested and booked at the county jail. Police have yet to disclose the charges, and Gizmodo reached out to the sheriff’s office for clarification. **What Gaggle does** Gaggle markets itself as a K‑12 safety solution that scans web traffic for keywords tied to self‑harm, violence, and bullying. Its system can capture screenshots and flag suspicious conversations, including those with AI tools such as ChatGPT and Google Gemini. The company states that, under the Children’s Internet Protection Act, schools have no expectation of privacy for students using school‑provided devices. **Criticism and privacy concerns** Privacy advocates argue that Gaggle’s routine monitoring normalizes law‑enforcement surveillance of students, even at home. Elizabeth Laird, director of the Center for Democracy and Technology, says the platform “has routinized law enforcement access and presence in students’ lives.” Many users report false‑positive alerts that raise alarms for harmless activity. **AI and mental health** The incident comes amid growing reports that chatbots are appearing in criminal cases involving mental health crises. A phenomenon dubbed “AI psychosis” describes individuals with mental‑health conditions interacting with chatbots in ways that may reinforce delusions. Several suicides have been linked to negative experiences with ChatGPT. Gizmodo reached out to OpenAI for comment, but no response was received. **Takeaway** While schools strive to protect students from violence, the use of monitoring tools raises serious questions about privacy, efficacy, and the unintended consequences of policing online behavior. As AI becomes more integrated into education, stakeholders will need to weigh the benefits of safety against the risks of over‑surveillance.

Climate Lawsuits Threatened by New Immunity Legislation

**Climate lawsuits face a new wave of immunity proposals** Republican lawmakers at state and federal levels are pushing legislation that would shield polluters from liability for climate‑related harms. The effort is part of a broader “counter‑offensive” that also targets climate litigation in courts, through state attorneys general, and in the White House. **Federal push for legal immunity** In late May, the U.S. Supreme Court agreed to hear a petition from oil majors Suncor and ExxonMobil. They argue that Boulder, Colorado’s climate‑damage lawsuit—claiming the companies caused harm by misleading consumers about climate risks—should be barred by federal law. If the Court sides with the petitioners, it could kill Boulder’s case and similar suits nationwide. Around the same time, Republican congressional leaders are moving to legislate immunity. Rep. Harriet Hageman (R‑WY) announced draft bills aimed at pre‑empting state and local climate lawsuits and shielding the energy sector, citing rising litigation costs. While details are scarce, the initiative mirrors the 2005 Protection of Lawful Commerce in Arms Act that protects gun manufacturers. The American Petroleum Institute (API) has publicly lobbied for such protection and frames its fight as a defense against “extreme climate liability policies” that could burden companies that supply affordable energy. **State‑level shield bills** Several states have introduced or passed bills that would bar climate liability claims against fossil‑fuel producers and trade associations: - **Oklahoma** – The “Energy Security and Independence Act” prohibits climate‑related lawsuits against fossil‑fuel entities and their trade associations. - **Utah** – A bill, now awaiting the governor’s signature, bars civil or criminal liability for greenhouse‑gas emissions except in narrow statutory violations. It is framed as protecting Utah’s low‑cost baseload power. - **Iowa** – Similar to Utah’s bill, it limits liability unless a clear statutory violation is proven. - **Louisiana** – The “Louisiana Energy Protection Act” preempts climate‑damage claims, even from out‑of‑state emissions, and requires state‑level approvals for any such lawsuits. - **Tennessee** – The “Tennessee Energy Freedom Act” also prohibits liability for greenhouse‑gas emissions from fossil‑fuel activities and declares a “right” to engage in coal, oil, and natural‑gas production. These laws echo concerns from legislators about protecting energy affordability, yet critics argue they prioritize corporate interests over the health and safety of residents in vulnerable communities. **Opposition and advocacy** Climate advocates and environmental groups have mobilized against the immunity push. The Make Polluters Pay campaign, the Center for Climate Integrity, and the Climate Accountability Project have urged Democratic leaders to block such bills. In 2020, House Democrats, led by Rep. Jamie Raskin, rejected a prior attempt to grant oil and gas immunity. Senator Sheldon Whitehouse (D‑RI) has warned that letting major polluters escape accountability would be “an insult to the American people.” In addition to the energy sector, similar immunity campaigns are underway for Big Tech, pesticide manufacturers, and private prison operators, raising concerns about a broader trend toward corporate blanket protection. **Why it matters** If these immunity provisions become law, they could strip polluters of the possibility to be held financially responsible for climate damages. This would also limit the public’s ability to seek justice through courts for harms such as rising sea levels, extreme weather, and health impacts linked to emissions. Former Washington Gov. Jay Inslee has called such moves “un‑American,” arguing that they would deny citizens their right to a jury trial. As lawmakers and industry lobbyists advance these proposals, climate activists are building a counter‑movement to preserve accountability and prevent a shift that could undermine both environmental protection and public trust in the legal system.
